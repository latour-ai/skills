# Side Letter Term Categories

The working map for extraction. Walk all five categories on every letter. The lists below are the kinds of terms that commonly appear — they are prompts for what to look for, not an exhaustive checklist, and not every letter will contain every item. When a category has nothing, the answer is "Not found," never an inference.

For each term you find, capture three things: (1) the right or obligation in plain English, (2) a short verbatim source quote, (3) an initial risk read against the rubric in SKILL.md. Preserve any defined terms exactly as the letter capitalizes them.

---

## 1. Economics

How the letter changes what the investor pays or how the firm shares in performance. Almost always commercially material and frequently precedent-setting, so read this category closely.

Look for:
- **Management fee discounts** — reduced rate, tiered rate, or a stepped-down rate over time.
- **Incentive allocation / performance fee changes** — reduced carry or incentive rate, a different hurdle, a high-water-mark modification, or a crystallization-frequency change.
- **Expense caps** — a ceiling on fund expenses or on the investor's share of expenses.
- **Fee rebates** — rebates, credits, or offsets against fees, including those tied to other fees the manager earns.
- **Founder / strategic / seed investor economics** — preferential terms granted for being early or anchor capital (e.g., "Founders Class," "Seed Investor" terms).
- **Special expense treatment** — bespoke handling of organizational, placement, or specific expense categories for this investor.

Why it matters: preferential economics are a top-tier MFN and precedent trigger and usually score High. Even a modest, "standard" discount is worth tracking because the next investor's counsel may ask for it.

---

## 2. Liquidity

How and when the investor can get money out — often the most operationally and risk-sensitive category, because better-than-standard liquidity can strain the fund and disadvantage other LPs.

Look for:
- **Redemption / withdrawal rights** — frequency (monthly, quarterly, annually) and any enhancement over the standard offering.
- **Lock-ups** — a shorter lock-up than standard, a waived lock-up, or a soft vs. hard lock-up distinction.
- **Gates** — exemption from, or priority ahead of, investor-level or fund-level gates.
- **Notice periods** — a shorter redemption notice period than the fund standard.
- **Suspension rights** — carve-outs from the manager's right to suspend redemptions or NAV.
- **Withdrawal frequency** — added withdrawal windows or special interim liquidity.
- **Special / most-favored liquidity treatment** — a promise of liquidity terms no less favorable than any other investor, or priority in a wind-down/queue.

Why it matters: non-standard liquidity can conflict with the LPA/PPM and disadvantage the broader base; preferential liquidity scores High. Watch for "most-favored liquidity" language, which is effectively an MFN scoped to liquidity.

---

## 3. MFN (Most-Favored Nation)

A right to elect terms granted to other investors. This is the single most far-reaching category because it can pull terms from across the investor base into this investor's deal — and can be triggered by terms in *other* letters you may not be looking at. Give it dedicated treatment (memo section 4).

Extract each element separately:
- **Whether MFN is granted at all** — and to what it applies.
- **Eligible-investor threshold** — the commitment size above which another investor's terms become electable (e.g., "investors committing the same or a lesser amount").
- **Excluded rights** — terms carved out of the MFN (capacity, regulatory, tax, ERISA, strategic, seed/founder terms are common carve-outs).
- **Election process** — how the investor elects, and whether election is all-or-nothing or term-by-term.
- **Notice requirements** — whether the manager must disclose/notify other side letters and on what timeline.
- **Timing** — when the right can be exercised and any deadline on election.
- **Future vs. existing coverage** — whether the MFN reaches only existing side letters, only future ones, or both. This materially changes the firm's exposure.
- **Confidentiality limits** — restrictions on disclosing other investors' terms, and how they interact with the disclosure the MFN requires.

Why it matters: broad or ambiguous MFN rights score High. The key questions for the human team are usually whether the MFN forces notice to/from other investors and whether a newly granted term must be offered to MFN holders.

## 4. Capacity

Rights to access or reserve investment capacity beyond the current subscription. These shape the firm's future flexibility and are frequently carved out of MFN for exactly that reason.

Look for:
- **Guaranteed capacity** — a reserved allocation in the fund or strategy.
- **Future investment rights** — a right to add capital on the same terms, or priority in future closings/capacity.
- **Strategy access** — access to a particular strategy, sleeve, or share class.
- **Co-investment rights** — rights or priority to co-invest alongside the fund, sometimes with fee terms attached.
- **Hard vs. soft commitments** — whether a capacity promise is binding ("shall reserve") or best-efforts ("will use reasonable efforts").
- **Successor / related fund rights** — rights to invest in successor funds, related vehicles, or new products.

Why it matters: usually Medium and important for IR/COO to track, but a hard guaranteed-capacity or successor-fund right can be High and precedent-setting. Distinguish carefully between hard ("shall") and soft ("reasonable efforts") language — the source quote is what settles it.

---

## 5. Transparency / Reporting

Custom information rights. These look benign but are the category most likely to create a recurring operational burden and to expose sensitive portfolio information.

Look for:
- **Position-level reporting** — disclosure of individual holdings, sometimes the most sensitive ask in the whole letter.
- **Exposure reports** — gross/net, sector, geography, or factor exposure breakdowns.
- **Risk reports** — VaR, stress tests, or other risk analytics.
- **NAV timing** — earlier or more frequent NAV delivery than standard.
- **Transparency into holdings** — look-through, full transparency, or managed-account-style access.
- **Custom reporting cadence** — bespoke frequency (e.g., weekly/monthly custom reports) beyond the standard investor reporting.
- **Portfolio analytics** — delivery of analytics, attribution, or data feeds.
- **Investor-portal obligations** — commitments around portal access or specific data on it.
- **Regulatory / tax / consultant reporting** — bespoke reporting to the investor's regulator, tax needs, or its consultant/advisor.

Why it matters: portfolio-level transparency that may expose sensitive positions scores High, as does any recurring custom reporting obligation (it needs a permanent operational owner). A one-off standard report is Low. The obligation checklist is where these earn their keep — every recurring report needs an owner and a cadence.

## Risk flags to surface explicitly

Independent of category, call out clearly when a term is one of these — they are the issues the human team most needs to see:

- Preferential treatment (economics, liquidity, or access better than the standard base).
- A possible conflict with fund governing documents (use the standard conflict sentence).
- Non-standard liquidity rights.
- Non-standard reporting or transparency rights.
- Terms that may trigger the firm's MFN obligations to other investors.
- Terms that may set precedent for future LP negotiations.
- Operationally burdensome obligations (recurring or bespoke).
- Hard deadlines or recurring obligations (these drive the checklist).
