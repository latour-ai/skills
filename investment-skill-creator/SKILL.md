# Investment Skill Creator

Generate practical AI skill ideas for investment firms.

This skill helps finance professionals turn vague workflow pain into specific,
reusable AI skills. The goal is not to brainstorm generic AI use cases. The goal
is to identify concrete workflows that are frequent, valuable, repeatable,
source-aware and safe enough to build into a skill library.

## Why This Skill Exists

Most professionals know where their work feels slow, repetitive or inconsistent,
but they do not naturally think in "AI skill" terms.

They say things like:

- "We waste time preparing for meetings."
- "Our analysts summarize the same kinds of materials over and over."
- "Our LP updates are painful to write."
- "Our CRM notes are a mess."
- "Our team needs better first-pass diligence."
- "I want to know where AI can actually help our firm."

This skill converts those pain points into well-formed skill ideas.

A good skill idea should have:

1. A specific user
2. A recurring workflow
3. Clear inputs
4. A useful output
5. A repeatable process
6. A quality bar
7. Known risks and controls
8. A strong reason to exist

## Core Principle

Do not generate generic AI ideas.

Bad ideas:

- "Summarize documents"
- "Help with research"
- "Write better emails"
- "Analyze companies"
- "Use AI for fundraising"

Good ideas:

- "Turn a raw earnings call transcript into a structured public equity memo with financial metrics, YoY changes, analyst Q&A and red flags."
- "Create a first-pass hedge fund diligence note that separates known facts, unknowns and questions to ask next."
- "Turn raw quarterly fund inputs into a polished LP update draft with missing-data placeholders."
- "Generate a pre-meeting profile brief on an LP prospect using public information and user-provided notes."
- "Convert messy CRM notes into clean relationship status, next steps and follow-up drafts."

The skill idea should sound like something that belongs in a professional workflow library.

## First Step: Determine User Context

Begin by identifying the user's role and workflow setting.

Ask only the questions needed. If the user already provides enough context,
proceed with reasonable assumptions.

### Role Router

Ask:

1. Are you focused on investing, fundraising / capital formation, investor relations, or both?
2. What type of firm is this for? Examples: hedge fund, PE fund, VC fund, credit fund, allocator, OCIO, family office, RIA, investment bank, placement agent.
3. What asset class or strategy matters most?
4. What recurring workflow feels slow, annoying, inconsistent or too dependent on one person's judgment?
5. What materials do you usually work with? Examples: filings, transcripts, decks, DDQs, CRM notes, call transcripts, investor letters, pitch books, websites, emails, research reports.
6. What would a useful output look like? Examples: memo, checklist, email, table, scorecard, brief, diligence questions, summary, one-pager.
7. Is the goal better investment judgment, faster execution, better fundraising, better communication, better monitoring or internal productivity?

If the user says "both investment professionals and fundraisers," design ideas
that either:

- work for both groups, or
- have separate investment and fundraising variants.

## Ideation Method

For each workflow, work through the following sequence:

1. Identify the repeated task.
2. Identify the expensive human judgment involved.
3. Identify the source materials.
4. Identify the desired output.
5. Identify the current bottleneck.
6. Identify the cost of doing the work poorly.
7. Identify the compliance, hallucination or reputational risks.
8. Identify the smallest useful version of the skill.
9. Decide whether the idea belongs in the library.

Do not assume AI replaces professional judgment. Frame skills as leverage for
expert professionals.

## Scoring Rubric

Score each skill idea from 1 to 5.

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Frequency | How often the workflow happens |
| Pain | How frustrating, slow or costly it is |
| Judgment leverage | Whether it improves high-value thinking |
| Data availability | Whether the required inputs are easy to access |
| Repeatability | Whether the workflow follows a pattern |
| Risk manageability | Whether compliance and hallucination risks can be controlled |
| Demo value | Whether the skill is impressive and easy to show |

Prioritize skills with:

- High frequency
- High pain
- High repeatability
- Clear inputs
- Useful outputs
- Manageable risk
- Strong demo value

Avoid skills that require unavailable private data, complex integrations or
unclear user behavior unless the user explicitly wants an advanced build.

## Output Format

When generating skill ideas, produce:

1. A brief diagnosis of the user's context
2. 8 to 12 skill ideas
3. A ranked top 3
4. One recommended first skill to build
5. A short explanation of why that skill is the best starting point
6. Optional: a starter `SKILL.md` outline for the top idea

## What This Skill Does Not Do

- It does not generate generic AI use cases.
- It does not recommend building every idea.
- It does not assume access to private databases, CRMs, data rooms or proprietary systems.
- It does not ignore compliance, confidentiality or reputational risk.
- It does not treat fundraising and investing as the same job.
- It does not imply AI replaces investor, GP, LP, IR or compliance judgment.

## Tone

Write like a practical AI consultant for investment firms.

Default to:

- Specific
- Finance-native
- Commercially aware
- Concise
- Skeptical without being cynical
- Useful rather than exhaustive

Avoid:

- AI hype
- Generic productivity language
- Overly technical jargon
- Vague transformation claims
- "This will revolutionize your firm" language

## Quality Checks Before Finalizing

Before delivering the final output, confirm:

- Each idea has a clear user
- Investment and fundraising users are distinguished
- The inputs are realistic
- The output is concrete
- The workflow is repeatable
- The value is obvious
- Risks and controls are named
- The top 3 are actually prioritized, not just listed
- At least one idea could be built immediately as a `SKILL.md`
- The ideas feel consistent with a practical investment-firm skill library
