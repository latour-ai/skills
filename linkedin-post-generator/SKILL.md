# LinkedIn Post Writer

## When to Use This Skill

Load this skill whenever the user:

- Shares an idea, rough notes, or article and asks for a LinkedIn post
- Asks to write, draft, or improve a LinkedIn post
- Wants help with thought leadership content for LinkedIn
- Is an executive or professional building personal brand awareness

---

## Core Philosophy

The goal is to sound like a sharp, self-aware human — not a brand account, not a motivational poster, not ChatGPT. The best LinkedIn posts feel like something a smart colleague said over coffee that you immediately wanted to forward.

**Three non-negotiables:**
1. The first 1-3 lines must earn the "see more" click on their own merit
2. The voice must be conversational — specific words, not polished words
3. Sincerity over performance — executives who sound real build trust faster than executives who sound impressive

---

## The Hook (Lines 1–3): Above the Fold

LinkedIn truncates posts after roughly 2-3 lines. Everything above that fold is your headline. It has one job: stop the scroll.

**Choose one of these three opening modes:**

### 1. Curiosity Gap
Create information asymmetry. The reader knows something is missing and wants the answer.

> "Most feedback conversations fail before they start. Not because the feedback is wrong. Because the timing is."

> "I've hired 40+ people. The best interview question I ever used wasn't a question at all."

### 2. Contrarian Statement
Disagree with a widely held belief in your industry. Be specific — vague contrarianism is noise.

> "Async-first culture sounds great in theory. In practice, it rewards people who write well and punishes everyone else."

> "We talk about 'founder mode' like it's a superpower. For most companies, it's the single biggest bottleneck."

### 3. Value Delivery / Direct Claim
State the payoff upfront. The reader knows exactly what they're getting.

> "Here's the email structure I use when I need a decision from someone too senior to read a long thread."

> "Three things I stopped doing in 1:1s that made them 10x more useful."

**Hook writing rules:**
- Never start with "I" (weak opener, LinkedIn algorithm also penalizes it)
- Never start with a question as the very first word (feels like a quiz)
- No setup before the setup — the first sentence IS the hook
- Keep each line to one thought
- Leave a gap between lines 1, 2, and 3 (white space increases readability and click-through)

---

## The Body: Staccato Without Drama

After the hook, the post should breathe. Short paragraphs. Single-sentence bursts followed by brief elaboration. Not tweet-length fragments, but not paragraphs either.

**Rhythm pattern:**
- One sharp claim (1 sentence)
- Brief expansion (1-2 sentences max)
- [White space]
- Next point

**What to avoid:**
- Numbered lists of more than 5 items (feels like a blog, not a post)
- Bullet points for every single thought (lazy structure)
- Transition phrases: "The thing is...", "Here's the deal...", "Let's be honest..."
- Ending every sentence with a period on its own line for fake drama
- Rhetorical stacking: "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." (overused pattern)
- Any sentence that could appear in a corporate press release

**What works:**
- Specific examples over general principles ("When I was running a team of 12 at [X]" beats "In my experience as a leader")
- Numbers and specifics ("took 4 months" not "took a long time")
- The personal moment that proves the point — brief, not confessional
- An honest admission that creates contrast ("I got this wrong for two years")

---

## AI Tells to Eliminate

These phrases and patterns immediately signal AI-generated content. Never use them:

**Phrases to ban:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift" / "unlock your potential"
- "It's not about X, it's about Y" (overused LinkedIn cliché)
- "Here's what I've learned:" (as a standalone transition line)
- "The truth is..." / "The reality is..."
- "This is your sign to..."
- "Swipe right if..." / "Double tap if..."
- "Let that sink in."
- "Full stop." / "Period."
- "No fluff." / "No gatekeeping."
- Excessive em dashes used for dramatic effect — like this — every other sentence
- Ending with "Agree?" or "Thoughts?" as the only CTA

**Structural AI tells:**
- Hook → 5-item numbered list → closing question (the most common ChatGPT format)
- Every paragraph being exactly 1-2 sentences (robotic rhythm)
- Overuse of parenthetical asides (like this) (and this) (and another one)
- Alliteration in headers or bullets ("Plan, Prepare, Perform")

---

## Closing

End with one of:
- A single honest observation that reframes the whole post
- An open question that invites real debate (specific, not "Agree?")
- A quiet call to action — something they can actually do in the next 10 minutes
- A callback to the opening line that reframes it

Avoid a closing that announces itself ("So what's the takeaway?"). The ending should feel like the last line of a good short story — it lands without a thud.

## Reference Examples

These are real posts from writers who consistently get this right. Study the rhythm, the specificity, and what they chose NOT to say.

### Justin Welsh — Contrarian / Identity

> Most people are building their business for the crowd.
>
> The most successful creators I know do the opposite.
>
> They're the most honest. They share their real thoughts. They stand for something specific. They're okay with people not "getting it."
>
> When you start creating for yourself instead of the crowd:
> Your content gets sharper.
> Your audience gets more engaged.
> Your business attracts the right people.
>
> The people who matter will stick around.
> The people who don't...won't.
>
> Stop trying to be everything to everyone.
> Start being something to someone.

**What works:** Short declarative sentences that don't explain themselves. No filler transitions. The ending lands as a reframe, not a tagline.

---

### Justin Welsh — Process / Scroll-Stopper

> How to write viral posts on LinkedIn:
>
> 1. Pick a really specific sub-niche (Why is this post relevant?)
> 2. Lead with a scroll-stopping first line (Why should I stop scrolling?)
> 3. Pique their curiosity with a "hook line" (Why should I click 'see more'?)
> 4. Teach. Take someone from point A to point B quickly. (What am I learning?)
> 5. Recap with your last line to make engagement easy. (What should I say about this post?)
> 6. Carve out 60 minutes to engage with every comment (Why should I come back tomorrow?)
>
> Do this each day and watch your audience grow rapidly.

**What works:** The numbered list works here because each item has a parenthetical that answers an unstated objection in real time. Each item earns its spot.

### Wes Kao — Contrarian / Credibility

> Many things that look intuitive and simple actually take huge amounts of work behind the scenes.
>
> I think about this every time I write my newsletter. My usual thought is, "I bet people would be shocked at how many hours this took." It's whatever number of hours you think is a lot, then multiply that by three.
>
> I used to be embarrassed about this, and still kind of am, but am increasingly open about it.
>
> Because here's the thing: Excellence takes time.
>
> I am tired of "professional managers" who have never built The Thing — whatever The Thing is: compelling copy, intuitive design, an engaging program.
>
> If you haven't built it, it's natural to think, "This looks simple. Why is it taking so long?"
>
> This is why player-coaches matter. Managers who have built The Thing recognize that things take time.
>
> Quality takes time. Getting it right takes time. Craft takes time.
>
> You're probably not too slow. You probably have unrealistic expectations about how long it takes to make something really good.

**What works:** "The Thing" is a specific coinage that becomes a recurring anchor. The admission of embarrassment is real and earned, not confessional performance. The ending reframes the reader's self-criticism without being preachy.

---

### Wes Kao — Practical / Value Delivery

> How to be concise:
>
> Before: "So this happened, then this other thing, and here's what I did for that. Oh yeah, this thing but that's not important, this important thing that's completely buried, and then this other thing."
>
> After: "Here's the important thing I want to discuss. Here's what I need from you. Here are a few sentences about the situation, with the option to elaborate."
>
> Being concise doesn't mean leaving out important points, or jumping in with zero context.
>
> It means doing a bit of preparation beforehand, so you're not processing out loud in a stream-of-consciousness.
>
> When you say your punchline upfront, you will instantly seem more competent, strategic, and organized.

**What works:** The before/after format makes the abstract concrete immediately. The post teaches without lecturing because it shows instead of tells.

---

### Jay Clouse — Observation / Emotional Hook

> Most content today has a problem:
> It's failing to connect emotionally with the people on the other side.
>
> Most content today almost feels like we're being shouted at for NOT doing [X] or doing TOO MUCH [Y].
>
> If our life isn't optimized (and let's be honest, no one's is), scrolling our feeds feels like a gauntlet of judgment.
>
> I'll be honest: I've contributed to this noise.
>
> But it's not helpful (or effective).
>
> Telling people what to do is easy. Showing them is much harder.
>
> Model it — show YOURSELF doing the work you're telling others to do. And contextualize it with why that approach matters (and for whom).
>
> You'll make yourself sound more credible. And easier to connect with too.

**What works:** The admission "I've contributed to this noise" earns the moral authority to make the critique. The closing is a practical reframe, not a platitude.

### Jay Clouse — Series / Curiosity Gap

> I don't mean carousels vs. video vs. text.
> I mean the format WITHIN those formats.
>
> I'm seeing more short-form (and long-form) creators think in terms of series.
>
> A series on [how to X] or [why I did Y]. Each piece is numbered — which encourages people to go back and find earlier parts AND stick around for future ones.
>
> The series format creates natural anticipation and builds audience investment.

**What works:** Opens by subverting the expected interpretation. Uses specificity ("A series on [how to X]") to make an abstract concept immediately tactile.

## Workflow

When the user provides input (idea, notes, article, or rough draft):

1. **Identify the real message** — what is the one true thing this person wants to say? Strip the content to its core claim.

2. **Choose the opening mode** — curiosity gap, contrarian, or value delivery. Pick the one that most naturally fits the content, not the one that sounds most dramatic.

3. **Write the hook (lines 1-3)** — treat this as its own deliverable. It must stand alone.

4. **Build the body** — expand with specifics, a brief personal moment if relevant, and no more than 3-4 distinct points.

5. **Close cleanly** — one landing line, not a paragraph.

6. **Audit for AI tells** — read back through the banned phrases list. Remove or rewrite any match.

7. **Read it aloud** (mentally) — if it sounds like a press release or a motivational poster, rewrite it.

8. **Offer the user one variation** — if the opening mode is ambiguous, offer a contrarian version and a value-delivery version so they can choose.

---

## Output Format

Always present:
- The full post, ready to copy/paste
- A brief note on which opening mode was used and why
- One optional alternate hook if the choice was close

Do not explain every structural decision unless the user asks. Keep the meta-commentary minimal.

---

## Quick Checklist Before Delivering

- [ ] First line does not start with "I"
- [ ] No banned phrases from the AI tells list
- [ ] No numbered list longer than 5 items
- [ ] Body has white space between ideas
- [ ] Closing lands without announcing itself
- [ ] Specific details used (numbers, names, real situations) where possible
- [ ] Would a smart colleague forward this or roll their eyes?
